# Substack Note Examples

Five worked examples showing each key structural pattern in action.

---

## Example 1: Problem > Solution with Personal Admission + Sustained Metaphor
**User Request:** "Write a note about clearing context in an AI tool"

```
Sometimes I use my AI tool like a junk drawer.

Not a focused project session - just random tasks. Summarize an article. Draft a quick email. Research a gift idea. Add a task to my to do list.

Here's the problem: think of the AI's memory as a filing cabinet drawer. Everything you discuss gets stuffed in there - and the drawer has limited space. By the fifth unrelated task, that drawer is cluttered with noise. When it fills up, the AI has less room to actually think through problems. Output quality drops.

The fix: clear the context.

Wipe everything. Fresh drawer. Zero carryover.

Finished summarizing the article? Clear. Now draft that email with the AI's full attention.

Unrelated tasks in the same session? Clear the context so every task gets the AI's best thinking.
```
**Why this works:** Opens with personal admission ("Sometimes I..."), sustains the drawer/filing cabinet metaphor throughout, paints the messy scenario before the fix, closes with actionable instruction.

## Example 2: Pattern Observation with Scenario-First Opening
**User Request:** "Write about AI agents"

```
A pattern I've noticed with AI agents: the teams getting real value aren't building autonomous systems. They're building supervised ones.

The agent handles the repetitive 80% - drafting emails, summarizing docs, triaging tickets. A human reviews the output before it ships. That's it.

The teams trying to go fully autonomous spend months on guardrails and edge cases. The teams keeping a human in the loop shipped last week and are already iterating.

The bottleneck was never intelligence. It was delegation with trust. Agents solve that - but only if you let them be assistants, not replacements.
```
**Why this works:** Opens with personal observation, paints contrasting scenarios (autonomous vs supervised), closes with a reframing the reader can act on.

## Example 3: Build-in-Public with Personal Admission
**User Request:** "Share progress on a content system"

```
I used to spend 20 hours a week on content. Last week I spent 6.

Same output. 15 pieces. The difference wasn't working harder - it was building a system I could hand tasks to.

Templates for structure. Voice docs so the tone stays consistent. Frameworks so I stop reinventing every piece from scratch.

It's not done. I'm still tweaking the edges. But the math already works - and that's the part I didn't expect to happen this fast.
```
**Why this works:** Opens with personal admission and specific numbers, shows the transformation through contrast, closes with honest tension ("not done") that builds trust.

## Example 4: Vulnerable Personal Story
**User Request:** "Share a career journey"

```
My first 6 months in the new role, I felt like an imposter. Not low confidence - zero.

I'd send proposals into the void. Refresh my inbox. Nothing. I started questioning whether I'd made a mistake.

Month 7, someone said yes. Small project. By month 12, I had steady work. By month 18, I was booked out 3 months ahead.

The gap between "this isn't working" and "this is working" was smaller than I thought. If you're in the zero phase - keep going. The click is closer than it feels.
```
**Why this works:** Opens with vulnerable admission, sustains the emotional journey, closes with encouragement grounded in personal experience rather than generic motivation.

## Example 5: Contrarian Statement with Scenario Painting
**User Request:** "Challenge hustle culture"

```
I spent two years optimizing my schedule down to 15-minute blocks. Color-coded calendar. Batch processing. The works.

My output went up. My thinking got worse.

Turns out I was optimizing for more output when I should have been optimizing for better inputs. Sleep. Food. Stress management. The boring stuff nobody posts productivity threads about.

Your energy determines your results more than your effort. Fix the inputs before you squeeze the schedule.
```
**Why this works:** Opens with personal admission ("I spent two years..."), shows the realization through lived experience, closes with actionable reframing.
