# Tax Authority Hierarchy and Confidence Thresholds

Use this reference when compiling authorities (Step 3) and stating the confidence level (Step 5).
The hierarchy determines how much weight each source carries and which confidence standard is supportable.

---

## Authority Hierarchy (Federal Tax - US)

Authorities are listed highest to lowest precedential value.

### Primary Authorities (Binding or Highly Persuasive)

**1. Internal Revenue Code (IRC)**
- The statute. Congress enacted it. All analysis starts here.
- If the Code directly addresses the transaction, cite the specific section and subsection.
- If the Code is ambiguous or silent, move to regulations.

**2. Treasury Regulations (Treas. Reg.)**
- Issued by the IRS/Treasury. Final regulations have the force of law when they interpret a clear statutory mandate.
- **Final regulations:** Highest regulatory authority. Treated as binding unless the regulation is invalidated by a court.
- **Temporary regulations:** Effective when published but expire in 3 years. Treated similarly to final.
- **Proposed regulations:** Not binding, but indicate IRS thinking. Useful as secondary source only.
- Citation format: Treas. Reg. § 1.162-1(a)

**3. Revenue Rulings (Rev. Rul.)**
- IRS position on how the Code applies to a specific factual situation. Binding on IRS agents but not courts.
- A directly applicable Revenue Ruling is strong authority if the facts closely match.
- Distinguish unfavorable rulings by facts, not by ignoring them.
- Citation format: Rev. Rul. 79-24, 1979-1 C.B. 60

**4. Revenue Procedures (Rev. Proc.)**
- IRS procedural guidance (how to do something, not what the law is). Binding on IRS.
- Relevant when the memo involves elections, method changes, or compliance procedures.

**5. Court Decisions**
Hierarchy within case law:

| Court | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| U.S. Supreme Court | Highest | Binding on all courts and IRS |
| U.S. Court of Appeals (Circuit) | High | Binding in that circuit; persuasive elsewhere |
| U.S. Tax Court (reviewed decision) | High | All active judges; most persuasive Tax Court opinion |
| U.S. Tax Court (regular decision) | Moderate-High | Single judge; published and precedential |
| U.S. Tax Court (memo decision) | Moderate | Not officially precedential but widely cited |
| U.S. Court of Federal Claims | Moderate | Non-Tax Court alternative; requires refund suit |
| U.S. District Court | Moderate | Non-Tax Court alternative; jury available |

**Circuit splits:** If different circuits have ruled differently, note which circuit controls (based on client's state) and address the adverse circuit's reasoning.

---

### Secondary Authorities (Persuasive, Not Binding)

**6. IRS Notices and Announcements**
- IRS announces forthcoming guidance or interim positions. Not regulations, but IRS will follow.
- Useful to show IRS is aware of an issue and has taken a position.

**7. Private Letter Rulings (PLRs)**
- IRS ruling issued to a specific taxpayer. Explicitly NOT precedent (IRC § 6110(k)(3)).
- Can be cited to show IRS reasoning on a factual pattern, but cannot be relied upon as authority.
- Useful when no other authority exists and the facts are analogous.

**8. Technical Advice Memoranda (TAMs) and Chief Counsel Advice (CCAs)**
- IRS internal guidance. Not precedent, but shows IRS thinking.
- CCAs are more current and published; TAMs are issued in the context of an examination.

**9. Legislative History**
- Committee reports (Joint Committee on Taxation, Senate Finance, House Ways & Means) explain Congressional intent.
- Useful when the statute is ambiguous and regulations are silent or unclear.
- Reference the specific report number and page.

**10. Tax Treatises and Law Review Articles**
- Scholarly commentary. Weakest authority. Use only to frame arguments when primary and secondary sources are thin.
- Examples: Tax Management Portfolios (Bloomberg), Federal Tax Coordinator (RIA/Thomson Reuters)

## Confidence Standards

| Standard | Threshold | When It Applies | Disclosure Required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **More-Likely-Than-Not (MLTN)** | >50% probability of being sustained | Tax shelters (always); recommended for significant uncertain positions | No disclosure required if MLTN met (for non-tax shelter positions) |
| **Substantial Authority** | ~40% probability of being sustained | Standard for most return positions; required to avoid substantial understatement penalty (IRC § 6662) | No disclosure required if substantial authority exists |
| **Reasonable Basis** | ~25% probability of being sustained | Minimum to avoid negligence penalty; not sufficient to avoid substantial understatement penalty alone | Form 8275 disclosure required to avoid penalty |
| **Frivolous** | <25% probability | No recognized legal or factual basis | Cannot be taken; exposes preparer to penalties |

**Key rule:** A return position requires at least substantial authority (~40%) OR reasonable basis + Form 8275 disclosure. Never advise a client to take a position below reasonable basis.

---

## Penalty Exposure by Confidence Level

| Penalty | IRC Section | Triggered When |
|---|---|---|
| Substantial understatement | § 6662(b)(2) | Understatement > greater of $5K or 10% of tax; no substantial authority and no Form 8275 |
| Negligence | § 6662(b)(1) | Failure to make reasonable attempt to comply; reasonable basis + Form 8275 avoids this |
| Fraud | § 6663 | Intentional disregard; 75% penalty |
| Preparer penalty | § 6694(a) | Preparer takes position without reasonable basis |
| Preparer penalty (willful) | § 6694(b) | Preparer willfully understates liability; $5,000 or 75% of fee |

## Disclosure Forms

**Form 8275 - Disclosure Statement**
- Used when: Position has reasonable basis but not substantial authority (non-tax shelter)
- Effect: Avoids the substantial understatement penalty when attached to the return
- Discloses: The item, the amount, and the authority supporting the position

**Form 8275-R - Regulation Disclosure Statement**
- Used when: Position is contrary to a Treasury Regulation
- Effect: Same as 8275 but specific to reg-contrary positions
- Note: Required even if the position has substantial authority when it contradicts a final regulation

**No disclosure required when:**
- Position has more-likely-than-not support (non-tax shelter)
- Position is adequately disclosed on the face of the return
- The item is clearly identified in the return

## Memo Completeness Check

Before finalizing the tax position memo, confirm:

- [ ] Controlling IRC section cited (not just "the Code")
- [ ] Applicable regulations cited with subsection (not just "Treasury Regulations")
- [ ] At least one primary authority (ruling or case) directly on point - or acknowledged as absent
- [ ] Most unfavorable contrary authority identified and distinguished
- [ ] Confidence level stated using the correct standard (MLTN / substantial authority / reasonable basis)
- [ ] Disclosure requirement addressed (Form 8275, 8275-R, or none required with reason)
- [ ] Circuit-specific case law considered if the client is in a circuit with controlling precedent
